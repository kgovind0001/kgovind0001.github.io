    Introduction to Construction Material Quantity Takeoff:- Counting the symbols

    A material takeoff ensures that a construction project is successful by providing a comprehensive overview of all the materials needed. It helps in:

    • Accurate Estimation: Allows contractors to provide precise cost estimates for the materials required, aiding in the creation of a realistic budget.
    • Efficient Planning: Helps in planning the procurement and delivery of materials, ensuring that there are no delays due to material shortages.
    • Cost Control: By knowing the exact quantities of materials needed, contractors can avoid over-ordering or under-ordering materials, thus controlling costs effectively.

    Electrical plan drawing with symbols descriptions provided in the legends.

    Creating an MTO involves measuring or "taking off" various components of a construction project to calculate the quantities of materials needed for each component. The process starts with the construction drawings, plans, and project documents. From these documents, you may quantify various building objects by:

    1. Count: For individual items like sink faucets, light fixtures, and doorknobs, the material quantity takeoff specifies how many of each are needed.
    2. Length: Along with the basic dimensions, the material takeoff provides the length for items such as pipes and cables (as well as the quantity of each).
    3. Area: The material takeoff for an area is typically measured in square feet. The surface area is commonly used for walls, ceilings, floors, and roofs for materials like drywall, paint, flooring, etc.
    4. Volume: For projects like a parking lot, the volume of asphalt per yard is critical information needed for the material takeoff.

    Challenges

    There are a few challenges in correctly identifying

    1. Plan Size and Resolution:

      • Large Images: Engineering plans are typically available as PDF files that can be converted into image formats. These images are usually of very high resolution, often exceeding 4000 pixels.
      • Small Symbols: Within these large images, the symbols used to represent different components are relatively small. Due to their size, it can be challenging to identify and distinguish these symbols within the high-resolution images.

    2. Orientation of Symbols:

      • Variable Orientation: Symbols in engineering plans can be oriented in various directions. For example, smoke detector may be rotated differently depending on its location within the plan shown above. This variation in orientation can make it more difficult to recognize and correctly interpret the symbols.

    3. Symbol Variation:

      • Numerous Symbols: There are hundreds of thousands of different symbols used in engineering drawings. Each symbol represents a specific item or component.
      • Multiple Meanings: In some cases, the same symbol can represent different types of items depending on the context. This adds another layer of complexity to interpreting the plans accurately.

    Legend of an electrical plan. Very similar looking symbols can represent different items as Waterproof and GFCI Receptacle.

    Incorporating AI into the process of identifying and counting symbols in plans can significantly enhance accuracy and efficiency. AI algorithms can be trained to recognize various symbols used in plans, regardless of their orientation or size. This automation reduces human error and speeds up the process of symbol identification.

    Classical Approach: Template matching

    One of the classical approach for the problem could be using template matching where we can use OpenCV or scikitlearn.

    The method uses two inputs, one of the source or main image and other is the template image. The source image is the larger image in which you want to detect the object. The template Image is the smaller image that contains the object you are trying to detect within the source image.

    Lets try this method for a plan image. To show the application of the method, we crop a portion of the large pdf of the plan and then we use two symbols as shown below.

    Figure: Template matching using OpenCV for the two symbols.

    The associated code which is just few lines long and the data to perform the template matching can be found at the git repo of blogs.

    The results of template matching for the first symbol are quite promising. Out of a total of 9 symbols, the method successfully identified all of them. However, it is noteworthy that the algorithm also produced 7 incorrect matches, which suggests that while the method was able to detect the symbols, it still had some limitations in distinguishing between true positives and false positives. Despite these errors, the ability of the method to match the symbol in the majority of cases is impressive. In contrast, the performance for the second symbol is significantly poorer. The algorithm correctly identified only 2 out of 8 symbols, and the results included a large number of incorrect matches. Interestingly, the method mistakenly matched straight lines to the symbol, which indicates that the algorithm struggled with certain features of the symbol.

    A key reason for the poor performance with the second symbol is its symmetry. The symbol is horizontally oriented, and the algorithm is not rotation-invariant, meaning it fails to correctly match symbols that are oriented differently from the template. This lack of rotational invariance is a significant limitation, as it prevents the algorithm from accurately detecting symbols that are not aligned in the same direction as the template image. Improving the results for the second symbol would require an algorithm that can account for different orientations, perhaps by incorporating rotation-invariant techniques or by pre-processing the image to normalize the orientation of symbols before matching.
